My research

Resolved star formation morphologies in quenching galaxies

I am interested in studying galaxy star formation on local scales with the goal of indentifying signatures associated with mechanisms that may be driving the overall quenching of the galaxy. This is possible in fields such as the Virgo cluster with multi-wavelength, high-resolution photometry to allow spatially resolved SED fitting and empircal derivaitons of star formation rates on small scales. With the advent of data from instruments such as Euclid, Roman, and LSST, this will soon be possible in certain deep fields as well.

Environmental quenching of galaxies

Galaxy environment plays a strong role in quenching, especially in the local Universe. I am interested in further understarnding which quenching mechanisms (e.g. ram pressure stripping, starvation, mergers) dominate in different environments. Particular I am interested in taking this beyond the traditaional cluster-field bimodality and looking at groups and filamentary structures around clusters such as Virgo, and working to map the cosmic web in deep field with data from instruments such as Euclid.

New Canadian-led instrumentarion

I have been involved with two projects led by Canadian teams to develop next-generation instruments for astrnomical surveys.

The Cosmological Advanced Survey Telescope for Optical and UV Research (CASTOR) is a proposed, space-based, optical-UV telescope with capacity to image at the resolution of the Hubble Space Telescope, but with 100x the survey speed. I have worked with the Galaxies science working group to show how CASTOR will revolutionize our understanding of the galaxy formation and evolution in large surveys out to cosmic noon, complementing the optical-IR data obtained with instruments such as Roman.

The Gemini Infrared Multi-Object Spectrograph (GIRMOS) is a novel IFU instrument being developed for the Gemini North telescope. As part of the GTO science team, I have developed science cases for early GIRMOS data that will allow us to resolve emission lines on sub-kiloparsec scales out to z~2.

Outreach

Throughout my graduate work, I have been an active member of the outreach team at the Waterloo Centre for Astrophysics. I believe strongly in the importance of science communication as a key aspect of working as a professional scientist.

Kitchener Public Library seminars

For the past couple years, I have hosted a monthly seminar series at the local public library, where researchers from grad students to faculty members from the Waterloo Centre for Astrophysics give a public talk on an area of astronomy of their choosing. These talks continue to be well-attended, with over 1000 total attendees to date.

Gustav Bakos Observatory

I run tours for the Gustav Bakos Observatory, located on the University of Waterloo campus. In addition to monthly public tours, I have hosted tours for school groups, community groups, and classes from the University. Often, I start with a short presentation on "backyard astronomy", or sometimes lead groups through additional activities such as "Parabolic Mini-golf."

Astro-Bubble

I have been one of the lead volunteers for the Astro-Bubble, the inflatable planetarium at the Waterloo Centre for Astrophysics. The portability of the planetarium has allowed us to host events across the campus, but also to visit local schools and libraries to give planetium shows for all ages. In 2025, I was one of three WCA researchers who embarked on "Astro-Bubble on Tour", a week-long trip where we brough the planetarium to 5 schools and a community centre in Northern Ontario to expand our outreach programming to more remote communities where students have less access to programming put on by post-secondary institutions.
